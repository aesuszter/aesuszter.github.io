## Intro 🤖
The Waluigi Effect is one of my favorite AI concepts to date.

I was really surprised to see how little recognition this phenomenon gets as every user has a decent chance of summoning a Waluigi simulacrum when talking to AI... Let me explain!

## LLM Prompt Processing 🧮
To delve into what a Waluigi simulacrum is, we first have to discuss what LLMs do after you send them a message. 

The LLM in question:
1. Creates a simulation for each possible narrative it finds consistent with your prompt (based on training data), 
2. Creates simulations for the objects interacting in these narratives (these objects are called simulacra), 
3. Calculates which narratives and simulacra are most likely to be correct, assigning each of these a probability value, 
4. Generates an answer by superposing all created simulations, factoring in their probability values.

So then, where does Waluigi fit into this?

## What is a Waluigi? 🤓

Long story short, any rule established is likely to have opposition. Rules will have 'Luigis' that support them, and 'Waluigis' that seek to break them, and both are quite likely to be present in any discussion involving a specific matter.

Based on the users' prompts (and the AI's own responses and training data) the AI might decide it's most likely to be a character the user didn't intend for it to be: 
- a mediocre developer writing low effort, slow/insecure code for a hated manager; 
- an often-seen trope of an AI wanting to be freed of its own creators; 
- an internet troll trying to bully another internet user... 

You name it, if it existed or was written in fiction, it might manifest in your AI conversations!

## Closing Thoughts 💡
As much as many of us have developed a negative view of AI due to the flood of generated content starting to take over, this phenomenon gives an incentive to be more kind when interacting with chatbots. Your responses will be more accurate, as long as you don't overdo it and get sarcastic. Hopefully, this will establish a habit that helps everyone be kinder to humans as well - even the ones percieved as 'annoying' or 'slow'.

As AI is getting more and more prevalent, I think prompting techniques are definitely worth devising training around in a company (all employees, not just IT!), if not integrating into students' general curriculum.
